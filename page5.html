1.5 Describing copyright and other constraints affecting websites



Copyright protects ANY piece of work regardless of who created it
and permission will be required to either use it, display it or distribute it.
Any complaint about copyright infringement should result in taking down the offending image or article until investigations can prove one way or another if there is an infringement.
There are legal restrictions on downloading and uploading software.
Again permission is usually required and licenses are usually acquired.
GDPR is another constraint.
It must strictly be adhered too else there are severe penalties.
GDPR is a digital version of data protection.
Personal and private information must not be available for just anyone and everyone to access at any time.
They must be protected.




1.6 Describing access issues that need taking into account - describe issues that people with disabilities may face when using web pages and how we can overcome them



For those with visual impairments, learning difficulties, hearing loss and more, there are dozens of unique challenges waiting behind every URL.
But building a disability-friendly site is a lot simpler than you might think.
Using alt tags with a screen reader will greatly help someone with visual impairment.
Providing subtitles or a transcript would also help.
Putting full stops in abbreviations is also simple yet effective help when using a screen reader.
Describing the links and making them standout using different colours is also a simple yet effective way of helping.
Just making clickable buttons bigger is a simple yet tremendous help for someone with mobility issues.
If your website is made disability friendly, why not create a page
that tells them about your features and helps them get the most out of the tools and features you are providing!!
Research into how your target users actually use their own browsers.
How they set up and use what they have.
This will really help creating something disability friendly and very useful and easy to use! [1]
As well as screen readers for the visually impaired or blind, for death people there are refreshable braille applications available.
Simply put, a refreshable braille display is a piece of hardware that provides braille output from your computer, smartphone, or tablet.
Buttons on the Braille Display or the keyboard commands on the connected device would move the focus to the next line.
Each braille cell on a braille display can show up to 8 dots.
In the UK, the Disability Discrimination Act covers web accessibility for blind and disabled users,
and it compels some business owners to ensure their websites can be used by everyone.
